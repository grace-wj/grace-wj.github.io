Cigarettes

2022-10-14

My best friend and I are getting matching tattoos soon. 12 years of friendship and now there’s a compulsion to drive some symbol of it into our skin. Maybe it’s to prove something to the world. Or maybe it’s because she’s 2,000 miles away and I want her here with me so bad sometimes it hurts. She’s taking a ‘gap year’ right now—to focus on her mental and physical health. Really, it’s because her family doesn’t have the money to send her to college. So, she and her high IQ get to vegetate in Cottleville, Missouri, cleaning dog shit 40 hours a week at Yuppy Puppy for a living. And I’m here. And the one thing that gives me the feeling like she’s not so far from me is the smell of cigarettes.

During recess, we used to pluck dandelion puffs, hold them up to our faces and blow their fluff away, then wish for her dad to stop smoking. Back then, I was at her house every weekend, and I still had issues with my breathing. Every Friday and Saturday night, I’d crawl up to the top of her bunk bed, close my eyes, and wheeze myself to sleep. I woke up from an attack one night and had to curl up on the porch outside for a while. Her mom got me a glass of water and I could tell she felt bad. I remember how nice it was to breathe in the cold night air.

I remember the circular burns on the old white couch I slept on when we got too big for the bunk bed. Next to it, how cruelly beautiful the glass ashtray looked when the sunlight hit it at a certain angle. Clouds of smoke lazily drifting across the TV screen while Carmen and I played Mortal Kombat or Halo or Tomb Raider until late into the night. Waking up in the morning and staring at grayish-brownish tar stains on the ceiling, recognizing familiar patterns. A childhood in a house with a tacit presence. And the smell of cigarettes.

He tried quitting once. He had a vape for a while, but one day I walked in and saw those white and gold Marlboros, stark against the kitchen counter. Yeah, okay. He doesn’t deserve the pressure. Working at a car shop, supporting a family of 6 on his own. He’s worn by work, but so kind. He pats me on the head and calls me his other daughter. He doesn’t deserve the pressure to smoke, or the pressure to quit. I don’t want him to get lung cancer. And I’m scared Carmen will get lung cancer, without ever having smoked in her life. I think she’s scared too, but there’s no point talking about it.

Sometimes I’ll walk past a stranger smoking on the street and a cloud will reach me and suddenly I’ll feel like I’m back on the couch playing video games with her. Wasting our nights away laughing and crying, hopped up on Monster energy and fighting to keep time from moving, keep the sun from rising. In high school, I’d show up at her front door when I couldn’t go home, when I was hungover and needed a crash. She’d make me chicken noodle soup and leave it by the bed. On those nights that went too far, I’d call her. I don’t want to be here, can you come pick me up. She’d get me from whatever house I was at, we’d put a box of toasted ravioli in the oven and turn on a movie and I’d feel safe.

Then the stranger walks away and the feeling fades. And I’ll want it back. The damn smell of cigarettes.
