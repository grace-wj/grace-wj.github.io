On Pleasure

Excerpt from a 07/27/24 journal entry

2024-07-27

I think this current phase of my life will be one of the most crucial and influential. I feel there is so much potential for change and such diversity of futures that I can barely think for more than 10 seconds without stumbling into an existential crisis. To keep grounded, I’ve been trying to focus myself on the core questions. What are my values? What gives me life? I feel like if I can answer these questions right, I might find everything else falling into place much easier. I haven’t gotten that far yet (truthfully, I think I need more life experience to really feel sure about these things), but I want to write down what I’ve got.

I love people, but I love more than people. If there were nobody else in the world, could I find happiness? Could I find meaning? I think so. I think part of the human experience is meant to be shared with others, and part of it is meant to be experienced alone. I’ve always felt that I possessed myself, and I think that relationship is what’s allowed me the level of life satisfaction I’ve always had. “I control myself” is the critical internal script. And when it comes to pleasure, control is everything.

If there’s anything I learned from studying drug addiction, it’s that moderation is key. The human experience is relative, and it’s subject to temporal distortion. Pleasure is meaningless if not compared against pain or boredom. The constant pursuit of increasing pleasure is never-ending. So if the goal is to die satisfied, then longevity is the name of the game. For every single thing that gives me pleasure, I’ve found that the rules are the same: not too much, not too often, with quality. A simple example: eating a donut makes me happy, eating 10 donuts in one sitting hurts my stomach, eating a donut for every meal gets boring, eating a bad donut is disappointing. The pattern is pervasive. I could assign a philosophical cause, but in reality I think this is just biology. In the end, dopamine tone is the puppeteer of the human experience.

So what are the things that spike my dopamine?

  • Physical agency. Whether humans have souls or not, we’re stuck in the physical world, and our body is our only means of interacting with it. I believe in the intrinsic pleasure of wanting to move and doing it. The extent of that movement, of course, is up to each individual’s discretion and preference. Some people don’t think about their movements very often. I have recently realized that it makes me happy to see what my body is capable of by using it to its full extent, and that physical limitations have the ability to take away every “non-physical” aspect of your existence. In short, I want to have a capable body that can move in any way it was naturally meant to and that gives me the freedom to do what I want.
  • Beauty. I don’t know why humans have such a capacity to appreciate art, and I don’t care. It’s probably the result of a complex set of interactions between biology, memory, and random chance, a state of being unique to each individual. The fact of it is that when I listen to beautiful music and look at beautiful sights, I feel happy. I tend to define beauty loosely as an external force producing pleasure for no good reason. Flowers smell beautiful, chocolate tastes beautiful, romantic stories can be beautiful. Really, physical movement feels beautiful (collapsing my categorization system here), but forgive me this logical error.

I believe that focusing my time and energy on these things that give me happiness will only add to my life. But pure indulgence is not an option, so what does “focusing” really entail? There’s a certain level of respect you have to have for your pleasures, something like knowing that you aren’t entitled to any of them. You don’t have them all the time, and you’re grateful when you do get them—if not, then you lose them. So discipline is paramount. But discipline is meant to control the runaway train of desire, not stifle it. Desire and pleasure are two sides of the same coin and experiencing pleasure isn’t passive—it requires some participation by the experiencer. You have to want the feeling and allow yourself the joy, otherwise what the hell is the point? Finally, I’ve found that the greatest pleasures are the ones that take time. Sure, I like listening to piano music, but actually playing my favorite piece is so much more enjoyable, and gaining that ability took commitment. There is something to be said for the return on investment of working to grow a pleasure for yourself.

  • Discipline—controlling your desire
  • Joyfulness—appreciating pleasure
  • Commitment—increasing pleasure
