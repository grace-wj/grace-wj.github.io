Thoughts on Korean Values

2026-05-14

Filial Piety

Is it a good thing to be obedient to your parents? Let’s think. It’s necessary to first define what we may consider a “good thing”. In a social context, I tend to equate “good” with “in favor of the human species” in a broadly utilitarian sense (as opposed to following any one religious doctrine or tradition). Operating under this assumption, I believe it to be clear that obedience to one’s parents is generally a good thing. In humans, a sense of fairness is deeply innate—there is always a subconscious understanding that favors ought to be repaid. For parents, this is encouragement to raise children well, a stark example of reciprocal altruism. For children, it becomes emotionally rewarding to pay respect to one’s parents from a place of gratitude, a behavior that feels most natural. Through an evolutionary perspective, a child’s obedience to their parents maximizes their chance of survival and reproduction because adults are smarter and more experienced than children. Of course, in real life there exist many different instances of child-parent relationships and contextual situations that challenge the theory, but the overall efficiency and beauty of humans’ natural filial piety is undeniable.

I’m American. Because American culture is something of an amalgamation of cultures from other societies/countries, it is hard to identify any specifically “American” equivalents to the concept of filial piety. The social behaviors associated with filial piety are commonly regarded positively; however, other aspects of American society interfere with the development of a strict dedication to the principle. Firstly, independence and individualism are integral values to Americans, and it’s easy to imagine many scenarios in which adherence to personal autonomy and filial piety would conflict. Additionally, there exist significant and comprehensive institutional and political support systems for elderly people in America: nursing homes, assisted living facilities, and Medicare. These likely emerged in response to the absence of widespread traditional filial piety, and as these structures persist today, there is diminished societal pressure for Americans to adhere to filial obligations.

In contrast, Korean culture has always been strongly influenced by Confucian values, filial piety being one of them. The strong expectation for adult children to provide for their parents is a cultural roadblock to political and institutional change. Besides this, it is obvious through behavioral observation the role that filial piety plays in many Korean people’s lives. To me, it appears that one’s dedication to their parents shapes—if it doesn’t entirely encompass—their reason for living a good life. You are expected to live for your parents, and when they pass you live for your children—at no point is it emphasized that you ought to live for yourself. In America, sometimes it feels like you’re born for yourself, you grow for yourself, and then you die for yourself. In my opinion, the best way to live is usually somewhere in between the two.

Modesty

Modesty in modern Korean culture has been influenced heavily by its highly collectivist culture as well as by the Confucianism of the Joseon Dynasty. Historically, the Korean social hierarchy was rigid and individuals were expected to act according to their station, compounding upon the Confucian philosophy which emphasized respect for authority and societal harmony. Ego (where there “should” be none, according to socioeconomic structure) was synonymous with impropriety and deeply discouraged. Not just impropriety, perhaps, but immorality. Carrying into the modern era, contemporary collectivism places value on cooperation and cohesion, naturally disregarding, even disparaging, pride, boldness, and individualism.

I feel that there is much to gain by being modest, for both the individual and society as a whole; however, there is a limit to the restraints that ought to be placed upon the individual ego. A community that goes beyond discouraging pride—instead actively punishing confidence—may find in its members a lack of motivation to succeed, innovate, or strive for excellence. And a community is, after all, only as good as the people in it. On the other hand, some amount of modesty is integral to the cooperative environment; modesty often goes hand in hand with humility, respect, and consideration for others, and a modest mindset facilitates growth and encourages continuous learning and improvement. There is undeniable good to come from shifting focus away from superficial displays of achievement and towards authentic connection and cooperation. I believe a balance between both modesty and pride is crucial, but in my personal opinion I prefer communities that value modesty first. True achievement never needs to be flaunted to be appreciated.
