On Inebriants

[FIXME update all dates] 2026-05-14

For art to exist, for any sort of aesthetic activity to exist, a certain physiological precondition is indispensable: intoxication.

Nietzsche, Twilight of the Gods

The balance is the acme of our Art.

It must be because I’ve always known it to be true, that I find it so inescapable: the idea that art, in the end, is driven by passion and by madness. As it is, I take note every time the theme emerges, and if [FIXME] Pendell is allowed to saturate his writing with impulsively dropped quotes, so am I.

The more ethereal, evanescent, more refined and sublime part of art is the seeing nature through the medium of sentiment and passion, as each object is a symbol of our affections and a link in the chain of our endless being.

William Hazlitt, “The Indian Jugglers”

Then there’s alcohol. Or ether, or opium, or glue. Nietzsche said ‘intoxication’, what does that actually mean? Apparently, ‘to be drunk’, or to be not sober. But sobriety is an empty concept, a null set only bounded by the adjacent regions of altered experiences. Another definition, perhaps, is the loss of reason—or better said, an escape from reason. And a surrender to the passions. Usually, such a surrender is bad for society, even worse for personal health, but outstanding for art.

A young musician of morbid sensibility and ardent imagination in a paroxysm of love-sick despair has poisoned himself with opium.

Hector Berlioz, in his program for Symphonie fantastique

Words uttered in passion contain a greater living truth than do those words which express thoughts rationally conceived. It is blood that moves the body. Words are not meant to stir the air only; they are capable of moving greater things.

Natsume Soseki, “Kokoro”

There is not much charm, I think, to creating in the absence of feeling (then again, I’m a person with a long-maintained Aestheticist philosophy). I believe that art that has been made to accomplish a goal, or art which gains value from the sparkly intellectual ‘meaning’ that some celebrated critic has chosen to assign to it, and that every viewer has chosen to absorb from it, is art that has missed the point entirely.

But then there are the brush strokes of a painter so moved by his subject that, even to the ignorant perceiver, they seem to burn with intimacy. Or eloquent expressions scribbled down by a writer seized in the grasp of a manic genius, high on pleasure or drunk with pain. Art created in the throes of passion, fantastically and unimaginably beautiful.

Because passion isn’t sane.

Roger Williams, “The Metamorphosis of Prime Intellect”

And art that forces you down on your knees and spreads throughout your soul until every fiber of your body seems to tremble with happiness or fear or devotion.

Oh bliss! Bliss and heaven! Oh, it was gorgeousness and gorgeosity made flesh. And then, a bird of rarest spun heavenmetal, or like silvery wine flowing in a spaceship, gravity all nonsense now, came the violin solo above all the other strings, and those strings were like a cage of silk around my bed. I was in such bliss, my brothers.

Anthony Burgess, “A Clockwork Orange”

It seems ironic to me that the effect of an artwork on a viewer can resemble that of a drug that may well have been used as a means toward its creation.

But honestly, even though they’ve been glorified by artists time and time again, I think the role that external intoxicants actually play in the creation of art is overrated. They’re a means, and only a means. No matter how any drug is characterized, in the end it holds no personality apart from its user.

If a man ‘whose talk is of oxen’ should become an Opium-eater, the probability is that (if he is not too dull to dream at all)—he will dream about oxen.

Thomas de Quincy, “Confessions of an Opium-Eater”

I refuse to believe that external inebriants are even necessary at all. Consider the overwhelming hunger of lust, or the devastating agony of grief. What intoxicating emotions humans can produce by nature alone!

That inspiration always comes from within, from rare moments of profound reverence, love, fear, or anger. Intoxication is a wonderful thing; life would be unbearingly boring without it, and to the artist it’s fundamental. And drugs have an extraordinary ability to evoke such experiences. In that sense, at least, inebriants are an ally indeed.
