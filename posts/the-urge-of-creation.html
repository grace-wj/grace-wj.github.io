The Urge of Creation

2026-06-26

Throughout my life, I’ve had this recurring experience that I want to examine. Every so often upon observing a scene, I’ll feel seized as from deep within me, suddenly, an urge ignites to do something with it. There is no pattern in the source—very often, it’s the most mundane of scenes: a stranger crossing their legs on the train, a collection of hair clips in a basket, a lineup of cars on the highway, or some other such still life of common banality, that strikes me in this way.

I’ve struggled to place the feeling because there’s not enough words to describe it. There’s admiration, like when you see a beautiful work of art, or a really stylish model, but undercutting it, there’s a critical momentum. An generative impulse, craving, or compulsion to either participate in it, capture it, or connect to it through creation in some way. Typically, I just end up staring, and maybe I’ll mutter to myself, “wow”. Sometimes, I snap a picture with the intent of drawing it at a later time. But very often, that later time comes and I open my sketchbook next to that picture and the momentum is just… gone. It’s the most unsatisfying thing.

What is this? Why is it that a passive thing as observation can take on such an active quality? I’d like to describe this encounter as a relationship between two worldly things. The experience occurs when one stops observing something in the world as a passive object, and starts engaging with it as a mutual subject. In rare moments, it can feel as if my consciousness is just simply present with another thing, not as the manipulator or the voyeur of it, but as an equal peer with it. My wondrous awe at it isn’t so much me asking myself, “what is this thing?”, it’s me asking some ephemeral existing thing “who are you?”. It’s a relationship. I take a picture in an effort to preserve that feeling of connection, but the picture itself reduces me again to the observer, and the thing the inert object.

I’ve come to view this experience in light of my broader understanding of what experiences cause forward momentum in humans in general. Beyond art, there is a distinction between relating as a way of nurturing a mutual connection, and relating as a way to propagate your own ego. Psychotherapist Terry Real describes what he calls “relational joy” as the profound, enduring contentment that comes from being in the flow of relatedness. It is a quiet, steady, deeper-down satisfaction that comes from simply being present in a state of connection. Most of the time, it looks like “nothing special at all”. He defines it apart from gratification, short-term bursts of pleasure, like from a quick ego boost. Gratification is deeply individualistic, and it has this tendency to leave both the gratifier and the gratifiee unfortunately and undeniably alone. Disconnected.

I’d like to use one of my favorite books, The Picture of Dorian Gray, as a case study in disconnection. In the book, the young and beautiful boy Dorian takes on the role of the passive object, captured in the eyes of two different artists: Basil and Lord Henry. From the start, Lord Henry outright exploits Dorian’s innocence for the pleasure of witnessing his own identity reflected back at him—seeing and hearing himself in Dorian’s ideas, behaviors, and proclivities.

He was conscious—and the thought brought a gleam of pleasure into his brown agate eyes—that it was through certain words of his, musical words said with musical utterance, that Dorian Gray’s soul had turned to this white girl and bowed in worship before her. To a large extent, the lad was his own creation. He had made him premature. That was something. Ordinary people waited till life disclosed to them its secrets, but to the few, to the elect, the mysteries of life were revealed before the veil was drawn away. Sometimes this was the effect of art, and chiefly of the art of literature, which dealt immediately with the passions and the intellect. But now and then a complex personality took the place and assumed the office of art, was indeed, in its way, a real work of art.

Talking to him was like playing upon an exquisite violin. He answered to every touch and thrill of the bow… There was something terribly enthralling in the exercise of influence. No other activity was like it. To project one’s soul into some gracious form, and let it tarry there for a moment; to hear one’s own intellectual views echoed back to one with all the added music of passion and youth: to convey one’s temperament into another, as though it were a subtle fluid or a strange perfume: there was a real joy in that…

Oscar Wilde, “The Picture of Dorian Gray”

The disconnect in Dorian and Basil’s relationship is much less obvious. Basil is innocent, he’s the painter who observes and nothing more. He has no desire to propagate his own image onto anyone or anything else, but he can’t help but bask in the beauty of an external thing when he sees it. He admires Dorian, but more than that, he idolizes him. He marvels at his beauty to such an extent that he reduces his existence to a symbol, a golden statue.

Dorian, from the moment I met you, your personality had the most extraordinary influence over me. I was dominated, soul, brain, and power, by you. You became to me the visual incarnation of that unseen ideal whose memory haunts us artists like an exquisite dream. I worshipped you.

Oscar Wilde, “The Picture of Dorian Gray”

He knowingly and happily positions himself as the hopeless, powerless observer, and Dorian as the ignorant object, trapped in his gaze. He may not have intended to harm Dorian, but he left him alone without a true friend nevertheless. Their relationship is a perversion of the mutual respect that ought to exist in an authentic friendship. There is no respect, only deference and tolerance.

Both of these artists are far more interested in objectifying Dorian to their pleasure than maintaining a true relationship with him as a friend. It’s clear that Dorian’s two most influential relationships in this novel provide him nothing but gratification under the illusion of care, setting a profoundly lonely and disconnected person off on a path towards destruction.

Bringing the thought back to where we began, creating art as a way to inseminate yourself into the world, to proliferate your own selfness asymmetrically, is a truly incestual cycle that, taken to the extreme, ends in abomination and ruin. Like the Ouroboros feeding on its own tail, it’s an unsustainable system. In thermodynamics, a truly closed system exchanges neither matter nor energy with its environment. Useful structure degrades as entropy increases over time.

We often speak of art as a channel to “express ourselves”, as if there exists some fundamental need in each of us to imprint ourselves onto our external environment. There isn’t. What we need is connection. A quote I read a long time ago, written by Haruki Murakami in Kafka on the Shore, is what made me think so.

…by Beethoven’s time people thought it was important to express the ego. Earlier, when there was an absolute monarchy, this would’ve been considered improper, socially deviant behavior, and suppressed quite severely. Once the bourgeoisie came to power in the 19th century, however, that suppression came to an end and the individual ego was liberated to express itself. Freedom and the emancipation of the ego were synonymous. And art, music in particular, was at the forefront of all this. Those who came after Beethoven and lived under his shadow, so to speak — Berlioz, Wagner, Liszt, Schumann — all lived eccentric, stormy lives. Eccentricity was seen as almost the ideal lifestyle… Though I’m sure living like that was pretty hard on them at times.

Haruki Murakami, “Kafka on the Shore”

Similar quotes purport that art is necessarily egoistic, that the desire to express yourself is a requirement for being a true artist. Identity is important, indeed (something has to do the relating), but I’m here to say that it can’t be the focus, because humans aren’t meant to live in such a way. Life is connection.
