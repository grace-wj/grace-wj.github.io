For a Diversity of Consciousness

2026-05-14

Introduction

In this essay, I describe the most difficult problem addressed by the science of consciousness and present an argument toward a combined scientific and phenomenological exploration of drug-induced mental states in pursuit of a diversity of human consciousness. Stanislav Grof famously wrote, in favor of research into psychedelics, that they “would be for psychiatry what the microscope is for biology and medicine or the telescope is for astronomy.” I would like to propose instead that they will be for consciousness what the particle accelerator is for physics. The ramifications of this type of advancement, however, would extend beyond philosophy; I envision a future in which the nature of one’s consciousness is no longer a given, but a choice.

On Experience

When dealing with the study of the mind, not all problems are created equal. In the broadest context, there is really only one essential problem, and that is consciousness; that is, the sum of the feelings, thoughts, and ideas encompassed in an aware entity—the subjective experience. Age-old rationalism has held that our own consciousness is the only thing of which we can be absolutely sure, and yet it remains the one mystery perfectly untouched by science. Within contemporary cognitive science, there is no shortage of complicated questions that need answering: How do we categorize and store information? What is the neural basis of emotion? What computational processes give rise to deliberate action? These are difficult inquiries that deal with difficult systems, to be sure. The human brain, with its billions of neurons forming trillions of information-sharing circuits and dynamic connections, gives rise to behavioral capacities so sophisticated they may seem to spring from an incomprehensible magic. So one might ask, what’s so much harder about explaining consciousness?

Yes, detangling the web of incessantly complex data structures that is the human brain is hard. However, armed with a faith in materialist metaphysics, there is no reason to doubt that the scientific method will be able to solve these problems in a few centuries or so. Reductionist procedures have prevailed in the face of seemingly intractable problems many times. The periodic table comprehensively represents the emergent properties of elements, just as the standard model does for matter, and DNA does for the gene. No matter the magnitude of complexity in the physical subject, we can confidently believe there exists a computational or functional model which suffices to explain it.

The key insight here, and what is so different about the mind-body problem, is that we cannot logically equate the physical processes that occur within the brain with the subjective aspect of the brain’s experience. The terminology used to capture this nebulous concept of the individual experience was put by Nagel in 1974:

there is something it is like to be

As far as empiricism can tell, there is no reason for the firing of a neuron to give rise to that what it is like experience, so—why does it? How are the two connected? This is what has been termed the explanatory gap by Joseph Levine, or, more commonly, the hard problem by David Chalmers. And it is here that opinions diverge. As Chalmers claims, “when it comes to the hard problem, the standard approach has nothing to say”, but that doesn’t mean that at this junction of neuroscience and metaphysics, there are no paths to take forward. Beyond renouncing the very existence of consciousness or stubbornly claiming to have already solved the problem, people have imagined many ways consciousness may still fit into our scientific model, or at the very least be added on top without conflicts.

For the hard scientist, theories based on non-algorithmic neural processing or chaos theory attract. But the most broadly appealing direction is toward a quantum theory of consciousness, for many reasons. On the surface, the two areas bear some obvious similarities: they’re both relatively new spaces of interest, shrouded in mystery and speculation, and they’ve both poked some holes in contemporary materialism. Some hold out on the idea that a classic physicalist explanation, yet to be found, underlies all of it. But the strangeness of quantum physics, having introduced an inescapable unpredictability into mainstream science, draws the excited attention of anyone with a slight disapproval of modern determinism, and vague conceptions of those novel ideas spread like wildfire among philosophers fighting to find empirical “evidence” for phenomenological hypotheses (it’s worth noting that not many people can claim expertise in both fields). On top of that, the apparent connection between consciousness and quantum mechanics known as the observer effect is undeniably fascinating and certainly responsible for generating special interest toward this theory. It describes the phenomenon in which the presence of a conscious observer collapses the quantum wave function of a particle and causes it to assume a definite form—the takeaway is that consciousness appears to have a measurable effect on physical reality. Intuitively, one feels compelled to shout “proof!” and hop on board, but a rigorous theory of quantum consciousness has a long way to go. This is essentially a case of attempting to solve one mystery with another mystery. Still, there is something to be said about the parallels that have been drawn between these two sciences.

The non-reductionist path puts forth an ontological solution, suggesting that instead of viewing consciousness as an emergent phenomenon of physical processes, we should consider it a fundamental entity of its own. A particularly controversial view called panpsychism holds that all things are actually a form of consciousness, that it pervades the world just as any of the other physical forces of nature. There are several well-known arguments put forth by proponents of these anti-materialist philosophies that help communicate the concept (which can be difficult to grasp), including the philosophical zombie thought experiment and the intrinsic nature argument. The knowledge argument, also known as Mary’s room, describes the genius color scientist Mary, who knows every physical thing there is to know about color and the mechanical processes responsible for perception of color, but lives in a black and white room. The question is: when Mary leaves her room and sees color for the first time, has she learned anything new? Panpsychists say yes, others disagree.

What one learns from an investigation into contemporary theories of consciousness is not just that there is no consensus, but that anything is possible because nothing is certain. The divide is discouraging. Some people believe that the mind-body problem can never be solved, and others, like Alan Turing, claim that the problem itself is trivial. But it is for the sake of human advancement and scientific progress that the attitude towards this particular issue should be optimistic. Chalmers himself encourages the curious mind: “the hard problem is a hard problem, but there is no reason to believe that it will remain permanently unsolved”.

Enter Psychedelics

It is that our normal waking consciousness, rational consciousness as we call it, is but one special type of consciousness, while all about it, parted from it by the filmiest of screens, there lie potential forms of consciousness entirely different. We may go through life without suspecting their existence; but apply the requisite stimulus, and at a touch they are there in all their completeness…

William James, 1902

There is much that can be learned about what makes a system work by breaking it.

Recent research on the physical mechanisms of psilocybin has shown (in most simple terms) that psychedelics achieve their effect by shutting down components of normal consciousness, providing some kind of freedom from what we know, and the most reliable method of accessing an altered form of experience; as such, these substances present a uniquely powerful way to study the nature of human consciousness.

The neuroscience of psychedelics is promising, but nascent. What we do have right now is a phenomenology of psychedelics; the number of documented narrations of the psychedelic experience, personal accounts of what altered consciousness felt like, is undeniably massive. Considering the current amount of scientific trials, there is almost no comparing the two. But more—the subjective descriptions are deeply moving. People speak of memories, emotions, growth, transcendence, and God. They attest to what William James called the “noetic quality” of mystical experiences: the feeling of certainty that one has come to know an absolute truth. (It’s worth noting, also, the “ineffability” of these events; an inability to communicate knowledge feels reminiscent of Mary’s supposed inability to know color without experiencing it). The very tone of these reports incurs further attention, and hints at something beyond neuroscience.

Traditionally, any kind of phenomenology has been considered quite separate from scientific investigation, both methodologically and with regards to meaningful discovery. Indeed, deference to the objective is necessary for a trustworthy conclusion, and the growth of controlled research will surely improve the status of psychedelic drugs in society. However, I predict that a breakthrough in this new realm of consciousness studies will require a copernican shift in the modern scientific paradigm, one characterized by a greater appreciation for the value of the subjective experience.

Altered States

To become more aware is your birthright. Denying anyone access to any facet of reality in the name of religion, science, medicine, or law serves neither the individual nor society. Whenever opportunities for self-realization are suppressed or in danger of being lost, there is a moral imperative to protect and restore them.

James Fadiman, 2011

In the pursuit of a deeper understanding of consciousness, the ability to alter the nature of one’s own experience is invaluable. In the pursuit of a good life, it is even more so.

There is a difference between knowing a system and being able to influence it. As it is, nobody can guarantee that an understanding of how consciousness arises from the brain will be found, but some extent of control over it has already come. Already, investigations into the possibilities of psychedelic therapy have shown favorable results for treatment of addiction, suicidality, depression, and other complex mental conditions on the rise in the 21st century. It is a characteristic of these conditions that they are defined only by the sum of their subjective symptoms, and so through the lens of physical study, have been notoriously difficult both to understand and to treat. There is an obvious leap to be made that perhaps the cure to these diseases of consciousness may only be found by study in the science of consciousness. It would be a terrible thing indeed to subscribe to an ignorant devaluation of a world of phenomenological information, and in doing so, disregard the potential for a quasi-evolutionary reclamation of human experience.

Equally terrible would be to withhold from any individual the freedom to better their experience of existence. A most compelling case study in favor of this view is that of dying, and the right to a good death. Psychedelics are able to better the psychological state of a person who is dying, and here we undermine the divide between the unhealthy and healthy consciousness, “because everybody dies, and nearly every person wonders, however privately, what form that death will take”—under the domain of consciousness, mortality itself is a disease. Perhaps the most controversial yet important view this essay strives to put forth is that the responsible use of psychedelics to better subjective existence should never be restricted. The right of the individual to alter their consciousness is undeniable, and any attempt to narrow the realm of possible experience under the guise of regulation, or even protection, is nothing less than a violation of that right.

The problem that the new science of consciousness faces is quite different from any problem science has faced in the past. The modern understanding of what it means to have subjective experience, and its importance in relation to anything but itself, is undeniably lacking. Moving forward in this new endeavor, it is vital to realize that the narrowing of our worldview will only work against us, and that there is more to the mystery we call consciousness that not only can, but should be experienced. This is what a diversity of consciousness means.
