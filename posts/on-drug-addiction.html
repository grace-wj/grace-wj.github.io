On Drug Addiction

2026-05-13

Intent is involved in addiction. Not that many junkies started out by one day thinking, “Gee, I think I’ll become a heroin addict.” In fact, those who start that way (“I just wanted to see what it was like to be an addict”) seem able to kick and leave the stuff with the same nonchalance as they began.

Addiction is something that really fascinates me. My conception of the idea has morphed dramatically over the years, and I’ve never once felt that I understood it. It seems the more I learn about the phenomenon, the less I know. Everyone is used to hearing these stories about drug addiction. But there’s always a difference between hearing stories and experiencing them. And, in this case especially, there’s usually an underlying plot. Drugs are evil, their users believe them the only virtue; they only cause pain, then why do people love them?; addiction is a disease, addicts compare quitting to grieving the loss of a loved one. There’s an obscured side of the coin that we implicitly fill in, but we never really see it. We see emaciated bodies, filthy crackhouses, broken lives, and we’re used to that. I mean, can you imagine a news report featuring the moment a meth user shoots up, right as he closes his eyes and writhes in pleasure? No, it’s abhorrent, that side. Leave it for them to discover on their own.

The truth of it is that drugs are good. They’re unbelievably good. And it’s the details kept in the dark that really show it. That lovely, simple happiness of cozying up in a blanket on a rainy day is the feeling opium can give—perfect calm. Molly is falling head over heels in love times 10, plus winning the lottery. Climaxes on crack can last for minutes, and watching porn on meth is better than the best sex in the world. Whatever wonderful thing you have in your life, there exists a drug that will make it so much more wonderful. All there is to do is give up your morals and your sanity and serve it for the rest of your life.

I can’t accept the thought that I won’t ever do it again.

There’s a realization that there is no satisfaction in an addiction to pleasure—the only conclusion of drug desire is the death orgasm. Plenty of people have chased that end; were they happy? What makes life better than a happiness-filled death? If the goal is heaven, why not enter the heaven (fentanyl) that already exists on Earth?

Here’s a thought experiment: if you could become an infinitely masturbating vegetable, would you give up your current life to do so? Most everyone would probably say no out of reflex. “Pain and journey is part of being human, if you give it up there’s no point.” Is there any actual reason for that to be true? Everything you want reduces to pleasure, and the pure happiness a synthesized molecule can give you is far greater than anything your brain can experience in normal life. Drugs are better than life.

But, I guess I don’t really believe that. If I did then I’d be a drug addict right now.
