The Line Grows Blurrier

Assembled from college club and program application essays written between 2021 and 2023.

2021–2023

Growing up, I was a bit of a tomboy, but in the geekiest sense. I didn’t like ‘feminine’ things, I didn’t like sports, but I went crazy for chess and Rubik’s cubes. Logic, algorithms, and defined rules came naturally to me. And I didn’t like to think in indefinite principles such as feelings—no, I much preferred to think in systems and central axioms. Every observation I made either fit into or informed one of my carefully crafted systems, and so I prided myself that every decision I made had a rationale stronger than “I felt like it”, or “I just wanted to”. It was always “this is most efficient” or “this leaves the most amount of future options open”.

Growing up, I loved to draw, read, and write. There were certain figurative elements, romantic themes, and eloquent prose that I found myself drawn to for no explicable reason. I didn’t know why I liked one famous author more than any other, all I knew was that I was enamored by the beauty of the art. For years, I explored the creativity of other artists and went through phases of focused obsession. During this time, I was like a plant growing toward light. There was no conscious system behind my behavior, I just let myself attract toward things that sparked joy within my heart, and I didn’t need more reason than that.

Throughout my early life, my love for art and my dedication to logic were two parts of myself that felt sharply separated—a dichotomy that could not be reconciled. There’s been a persistent thought in my mind that art is the only thing I’m really, truly, devotedly passionate about. And yet I’ve always deferred to my logic to dictate my life choices. Since I was young, my family and friends have pushed me towards art school or something of the same, but I abhorred the idea. I wanted success by society’s standards. Despite my obvious passion, art carried no value within my system. It simply wasn’t accounted for.

But, somewhere along the way, I started to account for it. And every day, the line that I set up when I was young grows blurrier and blurrier.

Interestingly, when trying to answer the question of why this happened, I came to realize that over the years I’ve gone through a significant paradigm shift in the nature of my introspection. By tracing the course of my development in retrospective, it becomes obvious what caused the change, but during the time it was happening I was blissfully unaware of the ramifications, of which my acknowledgement of the value of art is only one of many effects.

So what happened? It’s simple. Two things happened at the same time:

  1. I left my sheltered environment to attend college across the country
  2. I studied cognitive science and consumed new inputs on biology, psychology, and philosophy in mass amounts

As I learned more in my field of study—the science behind the emergent, effervescent side of my mind—I came to believe that every thought, feeling, and experience of the human consciousness, no matter how irrational, becomes logical in light of evolution. I came to respect its existence more and more. And as I encountered novel problems that challenged the simple systems that had been set in place by the static environment of my youth, I was forced to re-evaluate my perspective.

The separation of rational logic and irrational feeling when I was young really came from a lack of acknowledgment that I was more than just my brain. It truly never crossed my mind that perhaps, “I” was also my body and my soul. In my world, every experience was cerebral, physicality was meaningless, and emotions were unpredictable and unexplainable. As it was, I had little empathy for the people around me, I didn’t care for exercise, but I worked to consume knowledge like nothing else, and I was constantly analyzing and deducing information in search of “the truth”. In that world, art fell into the category of “irrational”. I couldn’t understand why I would prefer one painting or another, or why I would prefer any painting at all, so I simply ignored the fact that I did. In the same way, I liked to ignore my anger. My embarrassment, my attachment, my avoidance, my arrogance.

In class, I learned that humans can confuse fear with attraction because our brain mislabels the physiological state. We attribute the behavior of others to character and the behavior of ourselves to situation, without realizing how little sense that makes. We like what we’ve been exposed to before—even subliminally, even when we can’t consciously recognize the stimulus. We remember an experience disproportionately by its peak intensity and how it ended, rather than true recall. We’re systematically bad at predicting how future events will make us feel.

We are fundamentally, inescapably, painfully human. Not machines.

At the same time, I experienced several life events that truly challenged my erroneous, cocky belief that “I” was above my biology.

An excerpt from a 06/14/23 journal entry

I also think that another, bigger mindset shift has contributed to my dedication to working out. I don’t know how long, but recently I’ve been placing more importance on my body, as opposed to my mind. Or maybe in relation to it. I feel like my whole life I’ve kind of devalued the physical part of me. It’s always been about my brain, my intelligence. That’s me. But I guess certain events have brought to my attention how meaningful the body is—how it influences the mind, and is influenced by the mind. How it carries the story of your life inside it, shows the essence of your character to the world. And how it enables you, then limits you. Gives you freedom, or imprisons you. It has the power to control everything about you.

The first event is probably Alex’s heart attack. Just kind of shocked me into realizing how quickly your life can be taken: easily, from a simple failure of your body. It doesn’t have to be the result of your choices. It can just be a simple misstep in the workings of the machine that keeps you going. It’s so easy, we’re so fragile.

Then Coachella. Dancing then was probably the first time I really appreciated my body in that way. Like, just celebrating it, just me and my body. Together, without thinking about the judgement of others or the world around me. Just me and my body.

I’ve also started this new book, called The Song of our Scars, about pain. Specifically, about chronic pain in the medical industry. The author has talked a bit about pain in several lenses. The ideas that have been stuck with me: we don’t notice our body until it’s in pain, and chronic pain can erase a person. These two ideas strike me as true and profound, but they’re generally pretty ignored. Or not ignored, but I guess there’s just no need to talk about them, unless you’re a sufferer, or a doctor. But realizing what pain can do to you—not to your body, but to you—makes me want to take care of what I have.

And I like exercising! Like that night dancing, it’s another way to feel connected to my body, and indulge in that simple pleasure of moving. I agree with Krissy Cela: being able to move is an absolute privilege.

I feel that throughout this time, I was gently but firmly pulled out of the dogmatic slumber of my youth. I grew a deep respect for the “irrational” parts of my experience, and realized that there doesn’t need to be a line between those and logic. I love art. And just because I can’t trace every single step of the journey that started with the creation of my DNA at conception, was influenced by the stages of my experiential development, and encoded in each detail of the kaleidoscope of my life, that doesn’t mean there isn’t a reason for it.
