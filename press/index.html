hum

a pocket synthesizer for people who can’t read music

You hum a melody into the microphone. hum writes down what it thinks you meant and plays it back, voiced for two oscillators and a reverb the size of a kitchen.

~4,200 lines of Rust over a year of evenings. The hard part wasn’t the WebAudio graph; the hard part was deciding what a melody is when a human is humming it badly. The pitch tracker is forgiving the way an aunt is forgiving — it rounds you toward the nearest tune that loves you.

About 120 weekly users, most of whom can’t read music. The compliment I keep, taped above the desk, is from a friend who said her grandmother used hum to write a lullaby for her great-niece. The lullaby is two bars long and resolves on the wrong chord, which is why it works.

“It rounds you toward the nearest tune that loves you.”

— program note, evening of march 14

  • stackrust + webaudio
  • size~4,200 LOC
  • voices2 osc + reverb
  • users~120 / week
  • pricefree, quiet beta

slowcompiler

a compiler that takes thirty seconds to compile hello world

It compiles a small functional language that resembles ML, if ML had been written by someone who had just finished reading Bashō. At each stage of the pipeline it prints a haiku.

; lex/
let hello = "hello, world"
in print hello

; the lexer's haiku
;   the door swings open
;   four small tokens take their seats
;   the room is now warm
  1. 01 lex — tokens
  2. 02 parse — the small grammar
  3. 03 name — who is who
  4. 04 type — called “patience”
  5. 05 core — the lambda calculus, in lowercase
  6. 06 emit — a haiku, then bytes

I am interested in tools that make you wait on purpose. The wait is a kind of editing — it gives the writer time to take it back. slowcompiler is currently at the type checker, which I am calling patience. It is the slowest stage and the one I am most fond of.

~3,800 lines of Rust. Source will be open when the haikus stop embarrassing me. If you want to read the parser’s draft haikus before they are finished, write — they are kept in a notebook by the desk.

“The wait is a kind of editing — it gives the writer time to take it back.”

015 · WGWJ press · spring

weft

a jacquard loom driven by git

A small loom I built from a kit, a Raspberry Pi, and a git hook. Each commit becomes a thread; each project becomes a band of color. It finishes one scarf per orbital year.

Last year’s scarf is mostly grey, with three days of red — that was the week I rewrote slowcompiler’s parser. I wear it in November.

last november · one orbital year
